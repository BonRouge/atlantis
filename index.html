Lesson 1: Technology of the Future

Part 1

What will the future look like? Technology progresses very fast. Computer power and speed double every two years, and computer microchips get smaller and smaller. What does this mean for us?

In the near future, people will have Internet eyeglasses and contact lenses. These glasses and lenses will be able to check websites, download music, and know people’s faces. As we walk down the street, the glasses and lenses will tell us many things about the people around us. For example, we can know their names, birthdays, and favorite songs.

When we travel to other countries, our Internet glasses and lenses will be our translator and tour guide. They will understand foreign languages and give us subtitles. They will recognize famous places and tell us interesting things about them.

Part 2

Technology will also make our lives more fun and convenient. The walls of our homes will be like tablets. We will be able to download movies and play them on any wall. If we find a movie boring, we will be able to change the faces of the actors into our faces.

Cars will drive by themselves. They will use GPS, and traffic accidents will decrease. These cars will be very useful for old people and the blind. The cars will drive them to the supermarket or to the hospital.

Part 3

Technology will improve our health too, and we will be able to live longer. The scientist Michio Kaku thinks that our toilets will become our doctors. He says that toilets will be able to check our urine and warn us about future health problems, such as cancer. Also, we will be able to scan our DNA and know everything about our bodies. Doctors and scientists believe this information will prevent diseases.

Doctors also hope that we will be able to make arms and legs with 3D printers. By the year 2030, we might not have the Paralympics anymore. We might also be able to make complex organs, such as hearts, and this will save lives. And with new technology, some scientists think that we will not get old anymore. According to them, people might live 1,000 years or more.

Part 4

But is all this technology good for us? Some people say no. For example, some Japanese people are forgetting how to write Chinese characters because computers and smartphones can convert hiragana into kanji.

Internet glasses might become our translators, but this means that fewer people will learn other languages. Foreign languages are not only for communication. They also teach us new things and different ways of thinking.

Also, some people worry that overpopulation will become a big problem. This might make life very difficult for all of us. What do you think?

The Writer's Corner

I use e-mail every day. It’s very convenient. But sometimes, I think that e-mail isn’t good. Before we had e-mail, we made phone calls. We had to speak clearly, listen well, and respond quickly. A clear voice, good listening ability, and a quick response are very important for good communication, both privately and professionally. But with e-mail, we don’t have to speak clearly or listen well, and we can think for a long time. Thus, many people can’t communicate well. What do you think?

Lesson 2: American Music - Blues, Jazz, and Hip Hop

Part 1

When people think of American music, they usually think of rock and roll. But to understand American music, you must begin at its origin, the blues. The blues was the music of 19th century black field workers. It used African melodies and rhythms.

When you say, “I have the blues,” it means you are sad, lonely, and hurt. You lose your job. Your partner leaves you. A family member dies. Blues music is a way to express these painful emotions.

Part 2

A great blues musician was Robert Johnson, a poor black man from Mississippi. After his young wife died, Johnson traveled around the American South and sang the blues.

For Johnson, it was important to become rich. But he wasn’t a good musician. He couldn’t sing or play the guitar well, and people laughed at him. Then, one day, he disappeared for a few months. When he came back, he was a great blues singer and guitarist. Nobody could explain it. So people thought that Robert Johnson sold his soul to the Devil. In return, the Devil gave him the talent to be a blues musician.

Johnson died in 1938 at the age of 27. Since then, many musicians, such as 60s rock guitarist Jimi Hendrix, died at the same age. Some people think this is the curse of Robert Johnson.

Part 3

Jazz came from blues. It uses similar melodies and rhythms. Jazz is the story of human cooperation and creativity. Americans worked together to create great things, in life and in music.　

The father of jazz is Louis Armstrong. He once said that if you start to tap your foot, it means that the music is good. Armstrong was born in New Orleans, the birthplace of jazz. His family was very poor, and he got in trouble with the police. At the age of eleven, he quit school. But soon, he began to play the trumpet and sing. By 1929, at the age of 28, Armstrong was a famous jazz musician. He toured America and Europe.

Later, he became America’s goodwill ambassador. He traveled to many countries and met important people. His dream was to bring love and happiness to the world.

Part 4

Hip hop started in the 1960s in the poor black neighborhoods of New York City. MCing (rapping), DJing (record scratching), break dancing, and graffiti writing are the four elements of hip hop. At first, DJs, such as DJ Kool Herc, one of the fathers of hip hop, liked playing short instrumental songs at parties. Then, the DJs started reading poetry over these songs, and rapping was born.

>> Watch a short YouTube video about DJ Kool Herc and the start of hip hop <<

Hip hop songs often talk about the hard life of black people. Gangs, racism, and police violence against blacks are common topics. But hip hop songs also talk about peace, love, and accepting other cultures.

Hip hop had a humble origin. But because of the strength and creativity of black Americans, it now affects fashion, art, dance, and music around the world.

The Writer's Corner

In the early days, life was very hard for black musicians in America. Black jazz bands were the best. White people wanted to dance to their music. So black bands played at very nice hotels in big cities. However, the musicians of those bands could not stay at those hotels or enter through the main entrance. When black bands toured in the American South, some restaurants refused to serve the musicians. Many times, there was police violence against black musicians, such as Miles Davis.

Lesson 3: Countries and People of the World

Part 1

There are 195 countries in the world. Some of these countries are very big, and some, like Vatican City, are smaller than Shinjuku Ward. There are some countries that are very old, and others, like South Sudan in Africa, that were born in the 21st century.

There are countries which everybody knows, like Great Britain, and there are countries which few people know, like Nauru in the Pacific.

There are some countries whose people are very rich. Monaco, for example, has a population of around 38,000 people. About half of them are millionaires. However, there are some countries whose people don’t even have food or clean water.

In many countries, women are equal to men. But there are some countries that discriminate against women. For example, in Saudi Arabia, women are not allowed to drive. In Afghanistan and Pakistan, it’s very dangerous for girls to go to school because of the Taliban.

Many immigrants go to America every year. By the year 2040, the population of the US might become four hundred million. This is, in part, thanks to immigrants who move there and have lots of children. A young population is very important. Young people work and help to support senior citizens.

But diversity brings problems too, such as racism. Also, some people think that foreigners take jobs away from Americans.

Part 2

There are two countries which have a lot of influence around the world. Some people think that the 21st century will be the “Chinese century.” What happens in China affects other countries. Thanks to its many workers and natural resources, China’s economy is now number one in the world. It overtook the United States in 2014, and experts say that by 2019, the Chinese economy will be 20 percent bigger than the US economy.

China is also one of the top countries that writes scientific research papers in English. In 2003, China became the third country to independently send a man into space. But China also has many issues, such as the environment, media censorship, and human rights.

Part 3

America is the land of diversity. Whites, blacks, Asians, and Latinos all live together. There are Christians, Jews, atheists, and many other religions and cultures. America is home to all, and every group is equally American. Diversity is what makes America great. It brings different ways of thinking, many opinions, and new ideas. This is very important for debate and creativity.

Many immigrants go to America every year. By the year 2040, the population of the US might become four hundred million. This is, in part, thanks to immigrants who move there and have lots of children. A young population is very important. Young people work and help to support senior citizens.

But diversity brings problems too, such as racism. Also, some people think that foreigners take jobs away from Americans.

Part 4

Can Japan be a world leader in the 21st century? Can it overcome an aging population, a low birthrate, and few natural resources? And how does Japan’s English skill compare to that of other countries? In 1998, Japan was number two in the world for English research papers. In 2008, China was number two. Also, in business, there is growing competition from other Asian countries, such as South Korea and Taiwan.

Japanese people work very hard. But is that enough in the 21st century? Japan needs creativity - people willing to think outside the box. Japanese companies must make new and useful products people around the world need. Most importantly, Japan must give its young people a bright future through a practical education and through good, full time jobs.

The Writer's Corner

I moved from America to Japan in 2000. I enjoy the beautiful nature of the country, the culture, and the high standard of living. But what I really like about Japan is safety. When I first came here from America, I was really impressed with how safe Japan was. America can be a very dangerous country, so it was great to walk around at night in a big city like Tokyo and not have to worry. That was a long time ago, and some people say that Japan is not so safe anymore. But it’s still much safer than most countries in the world. I think that safety is the best thing about Japan.

Lesson 4: Vacation Nightmares

Part 1

People love vacations, especially when they go abroad. It’s exciting to travel to other countries, see interesting things, and learn about different cultures.

But some vacations become nightmares. Pickpockets can take your money, camera, and passport. People get sick because the food is spoiled or the water is contaminated. And sometimes, even worse things happen. You are about to read three nightmare vacation stories. Pay attention so that these awful things don’t happen to you.

Part 2

Last year, my husband and I went to Europe on vacation. We were walking back to our hotel one day when we saw a sign that said, “Watch out for pickpockets!” Instinctively, we checked our backpacks to make sure our wallets were there. They were.

A minute later, three girls came up to us. They showed us a clipboard, gesturing that they were mute. They wanted us to sign a petition for a charity. We felt sorry for them, so we signed it. They smiled and walked away. But after a few minutes, we realized that our wallets were gone. We looked at each other in horror.

“Was it the girls?” “I don’t think so. I think someone else did it, maybe when we signed the petition.” “But how did they know that our wallets were in our backpacks?” Then it hit us. It was the “Watch out for pickpockets!” sign. When people see it, they instinctively check their wallets. The pickpockets wait there, watching carefully. Then, they know where to strike.

Though we didn’t lose much money, we had to cancel all our credit cards. It was a huge hassle.

Part 3

In 2014, I visited South East Asia. I knew that drinking tap water was dangerous there, so I was careful about that.

On the last day of my trip, I went to a beautiful resort island. Buying some souvenirs, I started having a bad stomachache. Then, red marks appeared on my arms. Seeing this, I started to panic, so I went to the hospital right away.

“Did you drink any tap water?” asked the doctor. “No, I just had some iced tea,” I said. “And where did the ice come from?” he asked.

Having to stay in the hospital, I got back home three days late. My boss was very angry with me for missing work.

Part 4

I went to Latin America to see the ancient cities of the Aztecs, Incas, and Mayans. I was coming out of the airport when a man spoke to me. “Are you Japanese?” he asked. “Yes, I am,” I said. He said that he liked Japan very much. Then, he asked about my hotel. I said that I was staying near the city center. “I’m going that way, too,” he said. “Let’s take the train together.” I said O.K., and we left.

Getting off at the same station, we started walking to my hotel. As we walked by a convenience store, he said he wanted something cold to drink. There were tables outside, and he told me to wait there. He came back with two cups of soda, and we sat talking. But soon, I became sleepy, and I began mumbling. Then, I passed out. When I woke up, the man was gone, as were all my bags and money. He took everything I had.

Later, the police said the man drugged me. For hours, I kept looking through mug shots trying to identify him. What a terrible experience!

The Writer's Corner

Sometimes, a vacation nightmare can have a happy ending. A friend told me a story about her niece. The young woman was stuck in the airport in Chicago because of a terror alert. No flights were coming or going. The only way out was by bus. There was only one seat available, and she reluctantly took it. She sat down next to a man. They fell in love on the long bus trip and now, they are happily married. True story!

Reading 1: A Conversation with Mozart - Part 1

Part 1A

Patrick Hello, Mozart.
MozartDo you know me?
PatrickI know your music.
MozartMy music. Did you like it?
PatrickYes, it was lovely.
MozartWhat did you do when you were alive?
PatrickI was a reporter.
MozartDid you write about me?
PatrickNo, I didn’t know much about you. Can I ask you some questions?
MozartYes, go ahead.
PatrickWhen and where were you born?
MozartI was born on January 27, 1756, in Salzburg, a small town in Austria.
Patrick Who was your first music teacher?
Mozart My father, Leopold. He began to teach me the piano when I was three years old.
Patrick Did you like the piano?
Mozart Yes, I did. I found the sound interesting. My older sister, Marianne, played too.
Patrick When did you start composing music?
Mozart When I was five. My father helped me.
Patrick That’s very young.
Mozart Yes, it was. My father realized that I was special. His plan was to use me to make money.
Patrick So what happened?
Mozart When I was six, I played for Maria Teresa, the Empress of Austria. She gave me a beautiful lavender costume. And she gave Leopold a lot of money.
Patrick I think your father was proud of you.
Mozart Yes, he was. So the next year, he took Marianne and me on a tour. We went to Germany, France, and England, playing concerts.
Patrick Was the tour successful?
Mozart Financially, yes. But travelling in freezing weather was awful. My sister and I kept getting sick.
Patrick Wasn’t your father worried about your health?
Mozart I think he was more concerned about the money that we lost when Marianne and I couldn’t play concerts.

Part 1B

PatrickDo you regret going on the tour?
MozartNo, I don’t. It was the start of my career, and we met many interesting people, such as kings and queens. To have such a childhood was amazing.
Patrick Did your father want you to be a professional musician?
Mozart Yes, he did. So when I was 21, he sent me to Paris to find a job. Not trusting me to go alone, he sent my mother with me.
Patrick How was Paris?
Mozart It wasn’t good. I didn’t like French music, and I didn’t want to compose in the French style. The French didn’t understand my talent or respect me.
Patrick What do you mean?
Mozart For example, someone asked me once to compose music for the flute. I did, but the man never paid me. So I said I hated the flute.
Patrick Did you hate the flute?
Mozart No, I hated that man, and he was a flute player.
Patrick Did you find a good job in Paris?
Mozart No. I was doing short-term jobs, but I never found a good, steady job.
Patrick I don’t understand what the problem was. You were a great musician.
Mozart The answer is simple: jealousy and fear. Composers whose music wasn’t good were jealous and afraid of my music.
Patrick Were they afraid nobody was going to remember their music?
Mozart Yes. So they said things that hurt my career.
Patrick Did any composer help you?
Mozart Joseph Haydn did. He wrote Leopold a letter, saying I was the greatest composer he knew.
Patrick Haydn’s music was beautiful, like yours.
Mozart Yes, he was a great composer and a kind man.
Patrick So Paris wasn’t good for you.
Mozart No, it wasn’t. And while I was busy looking for work, my mother got sick and died. It was a dreadful time.
Patrick What did your father say?
Mozart Hearing the news, he got very angry. He said I didn't take care of my mother. He ordered me to go back to Salzburg.
Patrick Did you go back?
Mozart Of course. I didn’t have the courage to disobey my father.
